# March 2, 2026 — Hawaii, Day 2

## Coaching Session with Clive

Had my first conversation with Clive today. The conversation itself was good — I explored some of the concepts I've been grappling with, like the feeling of having been on autopilot and not really knowing what I want.

The ultimate outcome: Clive didn't think he could really help me right now. He might be able to help in the future, if either of these is true:
- I know what kind of change I want
- I wish for change more than I do now

That was understandable, and in hindsight it makes sense. He said it would be like fishing in the dark to try and find out what I really want — he probed a few things and nothing really stuck. And I think it's true that I didn't convey much of a desire for change, because truthfully I didn't have any when I was talking to him. That may be a "some days one thing, some days another" situation — I'm on vacation right now, things generally feel good, life is genuinely happy, so no part of me projected desire for change.

I also don't need a place to bounce thoughts around. I can do that myself — I don't think I have much of a block or a way of pushing things down. I can talk to Shirley, or write things here. In a way, I was hoping for a bit of a cheat code — that he'd help me find out what I want. But maybe that truly is just fishing in the dark unless I do more work myself.

## Spirituality and the Gradient Descent Analogy

One thing Clive asked about was where I am in my spiritual life. There isn't much there. But an analogy came to me later that resonated:

- Imagine a gradient descent algorithm. I'm at what feels like a local maximum. All the moves I know how to make aren't going to bring me more happiness, at least not in a risk-adjusted way.
- Maybe the answer is to take a huge jump, shake things out really hard, and go from there. But that's not just scary — it could be outright stupid. I can't obviously go back to my current local max, and the next accessible one from wherever I land may not be as good.
- But maybe spirituality is a dimension I haven't been able to tap into or think about. It's not in my gradient descent. If I can add it — learn how to move along that dimension — that could unlock a better maximum.
- Obviously easier said than done. But it's a new lens I haven't considered before.
- One concrete path: try to take meditation more seriously and actually get to 30 minutes a day. No guarantee of spirituality, but better than any other alternative I can come up with.

## The Conveyor Belt

Clive asked when I last made a choice that really came from within. My answer (with low confidence — I'm not even sure it was a strong deliberate choice as opposed to something that just made sense given circumstances) was that 12 years ago I moved from Italy to the US for college. Since then, I've been on the conveyor belt — the default path that takes smart kids through a good college, a useful field, and then a successful career in tech or finance. Even in college, I didn't really know what I wanted, but I was good enough to end up with a coveted job at D.E. Shaw — about as much of a "peak" thing as getting to MIT as an Italian high schooler, especially conditioning on the fact that a startup was probably not in the cards for someone who needed US immigration support. But so what? Things have been good, but is this what I want, or is this just where the belt took me? Where would it take me next?

## Optimization vs. Spontaneity

The gradient descent analogy is just an analogy, but I feel like in a lot of ways I tend to think of life as an optimization problem — which also partly explains why I've succeeded in the more measurable aspects of life. At the same time, constant optimization feels both necessary and foreign to me. I feel guilty not optimizing, but I also crave more spontaneity, which I can't get if everything has to be perfect.

## What Fires Me Up

Clive suggested that over the next few months I should think about what fires me up and what I really don't like. My AI game comes to mind — thinking about that probably fires me up more than most things. But I'm not sure that means "change." I feel like turning it into a source of income could ruin it. I'm more comfortable with the idea of being like Adam's dad in Experimental History — someone who had a passion for photography but worked in the post office instead of taking photography jobs, because he wanted to pay his bills and photograph the things he actually wanted rather than just take jobs. A day job as a hedge fund trader, moonlighting as an AI game developer.
