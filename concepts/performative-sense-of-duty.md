# Performative Sense of Duty

## 2026-03-01

The feeling of needing to "show commitment" by doing something visible during vacation or crises, even when the action isn't actually productive. Related to "virtue signaling" or "performative worrying."

- When Iran was struck, the instinct was to feel like I had to show commitment by working during vacation, even though I believed the proactive work wouldn't actually be useful.
- This pressure may be partly modeled from Greg, who rarely takes vacation and tends to obsess over work even when away. He doesn't explicitly demand this of others, but his behavior signals a belief in "the value of obsession."
- I don't feel a natural compulsion to obsess — it's more of a "sense of duty that has been put on me" than intrinsic motivation.
- After setting clear boundaries on the team call (available if truly needed, but not proactively working), the level felt right.
- Core tension: the desire to just be on vacation and take results as they come vs. the socially imposed pressure to perform concern.

## 2026-03-03

A live test of the concept — and some progress. Greg called asking if it made sense to work the week from Hawaii. Instead of defaulting to yes (performing duty), I thought it through, discussed with Shirley, and offered three structured options. Greg and Natalia picked the lightest one: stay on vacation, be available if truly needed.

- The guilt is still there — "sadness and feeling of guilt" even after the rational decision was clearly fine. The performative pressure doesn't go away just because you set a boundary.
- New dimension: it's not just about visible commitment to others. In a crisis, you can never quite do enough — there's always something more. The guilt is partly self-generated, not just socially imposed.
- A coaching text Shirley shared articulated this as "overinvesting in work to guard against anticipated loss" — the push becomes reflex, not strategy. The diagnosis resonated; the prescribed solution ("deliberate downshifting") felt hollow.
