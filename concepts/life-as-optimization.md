# Life as Optimization

## 2026-03-02

A tendency to frame life as an optimization problem — which partly explains success in measurable domains, but also creates tension.

**The gradient descent analogy:**
- Currently at what feels like a local maximum. All known moves don't improve things in a risk-adjusted way.
- A huge jump could land somewhere worse, and there's no going back.
- Spirituality / meditation might be an unexplored dimension — not currently in the gradient descent. Adding it could unlock a better maximum.
- Concrete step: try to build up to 30 minutes of daily meditation.

**Optimization vs. spontaneity:**
- Constant optimization feels both necessary and foreign. There's guilt about not optimizing, but also a craving for spontaneity that optimization crowds out.
- Life can't be perfect and spontaneous at the same time — the two pull against each other.

**Connection to other concepts:**
- The conveyor belt is partly an optimization story — always taking the "best available" next step rather than making a deliberate leap.
- The safe investigator identity fits neatly into an optimization frame: high-paying, intellectually stimulating, low personal risk. Hard to beat on paper.

## 2026-03-03

**A moment of non-optimization.** After a day of processing the work situation and writing it all down: "What else comes up? Right now, nothing comes up besides the sun and the wind and the smell of fresh plants. I can let go a little."

- This is the opposite of the optimizer's stance — not scanning for the next move, not solving, just present.
- It came after writing, not after meditating. Maybe journaling is doing some of what the meditation experiment is supposed to do — creating a container for the thoughts so they can be set down.
- Worth noting alongside the meditation experiment: are there multiple paths to the same "unexplored dimension," or do they do different things?

## 2026-03-04

**A different anchor.** After a painful day — the Singapore jet loss, the doom loop, the tightening — the landing point was explicitly non-optimization: "Things are okay not because I find refuge in my existing money or in Shirley finding a job or in the fact that I can cut expenses — but because I am a decent person and I belong in this world."

- This is a fundamentally different kind of reassurance than finding the next best move. It's not about positioning or risk-adjusting — it's about belonging.
- "We all belong in this world" came up again at the birthday dinner. Even while blue, hearing people talk about ordinary things was grounding. The optimizer turns off; the person stays.
- The morning entry hinted at the same shift from a different angle: "mindful habits" — noticing anxiety rather than solving it. The optimizer's instinct is to fix; the alternative is to see it and let go a little.
