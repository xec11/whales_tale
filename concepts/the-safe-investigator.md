# The Safe Investigator

## 2026-03-01

A self-description for the relationship with intellectually stimulating work. The core appeal of the job is the "investigator" and "debater" roles — thinking about markets, finding trading opportunities, analyzing and arguing ideas.

- The parts of the job that align with the safe investigator identity are energizing: research, debate, idea generation.
- The parts that don't — "executor" and "firefighter" — are draining.
- The bundling problem: it's hard to have credibility as an idea generator without also being an "actual trader" who executes. The investigation can't easily be separated from the execution.
- "Safe" seems to operate on two levels: the safety of a well-compensated career, and a preference for investigation from a position of security rather than high personal risk.

## 2026-03-02

**The "Adam's dad" model** — a possible resolution to the bundling problem that doesn't require unbundling the job itself. Instead: keep the day job as the safe, well-paying role, and maintain a separate space for the pure investigation/creation that actually fires you up. Inspired by a story from Experimental History about a man who worked at the post office so he could photograph what he wanted, rather than taking photography jobs.

- The AI game is the thing that fires up the most right now. But turning it into income could ruin it.
- Maybe the answer isn't to find a job that's purely investigation — it's to keep the trader role and moonlight on what you actually love.
- This reframes the question: instead of "how do I fix my job?" it becomes "how do I build space for what I care about alongside it?"

## 2026-03-03

**Firefighting week as a case study.** The current market crisis is a week that's purely firefighter/executor — no investigation, just reaction. Strong aversion confirmed.

- This led to doubt about the Michael & Suyash startup idea. Startups are firefighting-heavy by nature, with a built-in "you could always do more." If the aversion is to firefighting itself, a startup may be trading one version of the problem for a worse one.
- Emerging question: is the aversion to firefighting a property of certain jobs, or a property of me? Maybe with very clear boundaries on what I'm doing and what I'm not, the firefighting itself would be tolerable. The real drain might be the unboundedness — the sense that enough is never enough.
